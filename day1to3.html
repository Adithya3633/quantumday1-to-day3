🏛️ The Story Starts With Transistors

Let me start from the very beginning, because context matters. Imagine it is 1947. The world just survived World War II. Scientists at Bell Labs — a famous research lab in the USA — invented something called the transistor. A transistor is a tiny switch. It can be either ON or OFF. That ON and OFF is what we call a 1 and a 0 — the binary system that every computer even today is built on.

Now here is the beautiful thing that happened after that invention. In 1965, a scientist named Gordon Moore made an observation that became known as Moore's Law: the number of transistors you can fit on a computer chip doubles roughly every two years. And since more transistors = more computing power, this meant computers got twice as powerful, roughly every two years, for about 50 years. That is why the phone in your pocket today is more powerful than the computers NASA used to send humans to the Moon.

But here is the problem. Transistors cannot keep shrinking forever. Today's transistors are only a few nanometres wide — just a few dozen atoms. When things get that small, the normal rules of physics that we rely on start breaking down. Electrons start "tunnelling" through walls they are not supposed to cross. Bits become unreliable. Heat becomes unmanageable. Moore's Law is dying. And with it, the easy path to faster computers is closing.

Analogy to remember this: Think of Moore's Law like a highway that kept adding lanes every two years. More lanes = more cars = more traffic moving. But now the highway has reached the edge of the land — there is no more room to add lanes. We need a completely different kind of road. That different road is quantum computing.

In 1976, Jack Kilby's earlier work on Integrated Circuits (ICs) — putting many transistors on one chip — had already revolutionised electronics. The IC is what made personal computers, mobile phones, and the internet possible. It is mentioned in your syllabus because it represents the peak of classical computing thinking: make things smaller, put more of them on a chip, go faster. This thinking served us brilliantly for 60 years. But it is reaching its physical limit.

💡 Feynman's Revolutionary Idea — 1982

Now we jump to 1982. A physicist named Richard Feynman — one of the greatest scientific minds of the 20th century — was thinking about a completely different problem. He was frustrated that classical computers were terrible at simulating nature. Here is what he meant.

Imagine you want to simulate how a single caffeine molecule behaves — its electrons, their positions, how they interact with each other. A classical computer has to track every electron's state mathematically. The problem is, quantum systems grow exponentially. Two particles need 4 numbers to describe them. Four particles need 16 numbers. Ten particles need 1,024 numbers. Sixty particles need more numbers than there are atoms in the observable universe. A classical computer cannot possibly simulate a medium-sized molecule accurately. It would run out of memory before it even started.

Feynman's insight was startlingly simple: "If you want to simulate nature, use nature itself." Since molecules are quantum systems, use a quantum system to simulate them. A quantum computer, being quantum, would naturally handle quantum behaviour without exponential blowup. This idea — that quantum computers could simulate things classical computers simply cannot — is the founding concept of the entire field.

Why this matters for your exam: If asked "Who first proposed quantum computing and why?" — the answer is Richard Feynman in 1982, and the reason is to simulate quantum physical systems that are impossible to simulate on classical computers.

⚖️ Classical vs Quantum — What's Actually Different?

Before we talk about quantum phenomena, let me make sure you understand the fundamental difference between how classical and quantum computers work. This is one of the most commonly tested areas.

Classical Computer
Unit: Bit — either 0 or 1. Never both.
Processing: One state at a time. Sequential or parallel but always definite.
Nature: Deterministic — same input always gives same output.
Memory scales: Linearly — 10 bits stores 10 values.
Best for: Email, gaming, spreadsheets, web browsing, video.
Limitation: Exponential problems — molecule simulation, optimisation with billions of variables — are forever out of reach.
Quantum Computer
Unit: Qubit — can be 0, 1, or a superposition of both.
Processing: Explores many states simultaneously through superposition.
Nature: Probabilistic — measurement gives a probability distribution of results.
Memory scales: Exponentially — 10 qubits can represent 2¹⁰ = 1024 states simultaneously.
Best for: Cryptography, drug discovery, optimisation, quantum simulation, ML.
Limitation: Fragile — noise and decoherence destroy quantum states. Not good for everyday tasks classical computers handle well.
Common misconception to avoid in your exam: Quantum computers are NOT just faster classical computers. They are not going to replace your laptop. They solve a completely different category of problems — ones where the number of possibilities is so astronomically large that classical computers can never check them all.

🌊 The 3 Quantum Phenomena — The Heart of Everything

Everything in quantum computing — every algorithm, every speedup, every advantage — comes from three fundamental quantum mechanical phenomena. These are not tricks or approximations. They are deep properties of nature that classical physics simply does not have. Understand these three things deeply and half your exam is done.

🌀
1. Superposition
A qubit can exist as 0 AND 1 at the same time — until it is measured. This is not a metaphor. It is a physical reality. The qubit is literally in both states simultaneously, with some probability of being found as 0 and some probability of being 1. Only measurement forces it to choose.
🔗
2. Entanglement
Two qubits can be "entangled" — linked in a way that knowing the state of one instantly tells you the state of the other, no matter how far apart they are. Einstein hated this and called it "spooky action at a distance." But experiments have confirmed it is real. Used in quantum teleportation and secure communication.
〰️
3. Interference
Quantum states have phases, like waves. Correct answers can be made to constructively interfere (amplified) while wrong answers destructively interfere (cancelled), like noise-cancelling headphones. This is how quantum algorithms selectively amplify the right answer out of a sea of possibilities.

Let me expand on each with analogies because these concepts are not intuitive at all.

Superposition analogy: Imagine spinning a coin on a table. While it is spinning, it is neither heads nor tails — it is a combination of both. Only when you catch it (measure it) does it become definitively one or the other. A qubit in superposition is exactly like that spinning coin. The difference: a quantum computer can work with the spinning coin before it lands, exploring both possibilities at once.
Entanglement analogy: Imagine you have a pair of magic gloves. You take one glove to Hyderabad and send the other to New York without looking at either. The moment you open the box in Hyderabad and see "left glove," you instantly know the one in New York is the "right glove" — without any communication. Entanglement is like this but at the quantum level, and the "gloves" don't have a definite value until you look. The correlation is perfect and instantaneous, but cannot be used to send information faster than light.
Interference analogy: Think about noise-cancelling headphones. They generate a sound wave that is the exact opposite of the background noise. When the two waves meet, they cancel each other out — destructive interference. Quantum algorithms use the same wave-like mathematics. Wrong answers get cancelled by destructive interference; the correct answer gets amplified by constructive interference. This is why the right answer "pops out" at the end of a quantum computation.

📋 DiVincenzo's 5 Criteria — What Makes a Real Quantum Computer?

In 2000, David DiVincenzo at IBM published a landmark paper defining exactly what any physical system must be able to do in order to function as a quantum computer. These 5 criteria are now the universal checklist. Every quantum hardware company — IBM, Google, IonQ — is evaluated against these criteria. This is extremely likely to appear in your exam either as an MCQ or a list question.

1
Scalable physical qubits
You need a physical system where you can define individual qubits, and where you can add more qubits as needed. You cannot build a useful quantum computer with just 5 qubits — you need thousands or millions for real problems. The system must be scalable. Today, IBM has over 1000-qubit chips but making them reliable is the challenge. Scalability is the hardest unsolved problem in quantum hardware right now.
2
Initialisation to a known state
Before every computation, you must be able to reset all qubits to a known, simple starting state — typically |0⟩. Think of it like clearing a whiteboard before writing. If the whiteboard already has random scribbles, you cannot reliably write new information on it. Quantum computers must start from a clean slate. This is done using special cooling procedures and microwave pulses depending on the hardware type.
3
Long coherence times relative to gate times
A qubit must stay "quantum" (maintain its superposition and entanglement) long enough to complete all the gate operations in a computation. Decoherence is the enemy — it is the process by which a qubit loses its quantum properties due to environmental noise (heat, vibrations, electromagnetic fields). The coherence time must be much longer than the time needed to apply gates. Today's best superconducting qubits have coherence times of hundreds of microseconds — which sounds short but is actually thousands of gate operations long.
4
Universal set of quantum gates
A universal set of operations that can implement any quantum computation. In classical computing, any boolean function can be built from NAND gates alone. In quantum computing, you need a small set of gates — typically Hadamard (H), Phase (S), T gate, and CNOT — that together can implement any quantum algorithm. "Universal" means you are not limited — any possible quantum computation can be built from these building blocks.
5
Qubit-specific measurement capability
You must be able to measure individual qubits at the end of a computation to read out the result. Crucially, you should be able to measure one qubit without disturbing its neighbours. This is harder than it sounds — quantum systems are so delicate that even looking at them changes them. High-fidelity measurement (measuring correctly more than 99% of the time) is a major engineering challenge.
Memory trick — "SILUM": Scalable qubits · Initialisation · Long coherence · Universal gates · Measurement. Say it 5 times: SILUM, SILUM, SILUM, SILUM, SILUM.
DiVincenzo also added 2 bonus criteria for quantum communication (not always tested but good to know): 6) Ability to interconvert stationary and flying qubits (convert between stored and transmitted qubits), and 7) Ability to faithfully transmit flying qubits between locations. These are relevant to the quantum internet topics in Module 2.

⚛️ What Exactly IS a Qubit?

Yesterday you learned that a qubit can be 0 and 1 at the same time. Today let's go deeper — what is a qubit physically? How does it exist in the real world?

A qubit is any quantum two-level system — something that can be in one of two quantum states, which we call |0⟩ and |1⟩. The physical implementation varies by company, but the quantum mathematics is identical. Here are the main types:

📐 The Language of Qubits — Dirac Notation

Quantum mechanics has its own notation called Dirac notation or bra-ket notation, invented by physicist Paul Dirac. It looks scary at first but it is just a clean way of writing quantum states. You do not need to do the math — just understand what the symbols mean.

Ket notation: A quantum state is written as |ψ⟩ (read: "ket psi"). The vertical bar and angle bracket together form the "ket." So |0⟩ means "qubit in state 0" and |1⟩ means "qubit in state 1."

A general qubit state in superposition is written as:

|ψ⟩ = α|0⟩ + β|1⟩

Where:
α = amplitude for state 0 (a complex number)
β = amplitude for state 1 (a complex number)

The rule — probabilities must add to 100%:
|α|² + |β|² = 1

When you MEASURE this qubit:
Probability of getting 0 = |α|²
Probability of getting 1 = |β|²
Example: If α = β = 1/√2, then |α|² = 0.5 and |β|² = 0.5. So the qubit has exactly 50% chance of being measured as 0 and 50% as 1. This is called equal superposition — the quantum equivalent of a perfectly fair coin. The Hadamard gate creates this exact state from |0⟩.

Two important "basis states" (pure, non-superposition states) are:

|0⟩ = 1·|0⟩ + 0·|1⟩ → 100% chance of measuring 0. α=1, β=0
|1⟩ = 0·|0⟩ + 1·|1⟩ → 100% chance of measuring 1. α=0, β=1

🌐 The Bloch Sphere — Seeing a Qubit

A classical bit lives on a number line with only two points: 0 and 1. A qubit lives on the surface of a sphere — the Bloch sphere. Every point on the surface of this sphere represents a valid quantum state. This is a powerful visual tool.

⬆️
North Pole = |0⟩
The "ground state" or computational zero. When you initialise a qubit before computation, you put it at the north pole.
⬇️
South Pole = |1⟩
The excited state or computational one. The Pauli-X gate flips a qubit from north to south pole — like a classical NOT gate.
➡️
Equator = Equal Superposition
Any point on the equator represents 50/50 superposition — measuring gives 0 or 1 with equal probability. The Hadamard gate moves a qubit from north pole to the equator.
🔄
Gates = Rotations
Every quantum gate is simply a rotation of the Bloch sphere. H gate rotates from pole to equator. X gate rotates 180° around X-axis. Z gate rotates 180° around Z-axis.
Key insight for your exam: The Bloch sphere gives a qubit an infinite number of possible states (every point on the sphere's surface). But when you measure it, it collapses to just one of two points — north (0) or south (1). The measurement throws away all the rich quantum information and leaves you with just a classical bit. That is why you run quantum circuits many times — to reconstruct the probability distribution.

🚪 Quantum Gates — Every Single One You Need to Know

Quantum gates are the operations that manipulate qubits. They are the quantum equivalent of classical logic gates (AND, OR, NOT). There is one crucial difference from classical gates: all quantum gates are reversible. You can always undo a quantum gate. Classical AND gate is not reversible (knowing the output 0 doesn't tell you if inputs were 00, 01, or 10). This reversibility is a deep consequence of quantum mechanics and connects to energy efficiency in computation.

Let me explain the CNOT gate in extra detail because it is the gate that creates entanglement — the most powerful quantum resource.

CNOT gate truth table — memorise this:

Control=0, Target=0 → Output: 00 (nothing changes)
Control=0, Target=1 → Output: 01 (nothing changes)
Control=1, Target=0 → Output: 11 (target flipped)
Control=1, Target=1 → Output: 10 (target flipped)

Rule: If control is |1⟩, flip target. If control is |0⟩, leave target alone.

Now here is the magical part. What happens when you apply Hadamard first, then CNOT? Let's trace through it step by step:

Creating a Bell state (entangled pair) — step by step:

Start: |00⟩ (both qubits are 0)

Step 1: Apply H to qubit 0:
|00⟩ → (|0⟩+|1⟩)/√2 ⊗ |0⟩ = (|00⟩+|10⟩)/√2
Now qubit 0 is in superposition, qubit 1 is still |0⟩

Step 2: Apply CNOT (qubit 0 = control, qubit 1 = target):
|00⟩ → |00⟩ (control=0, target unchanged)
|10⟩ → |11⟩ (control=1, target flipped)

Result: (|00⟩+|11⟩)/√2

This is a BELL STATE — perfectly entangled!
Measure qubit 0 → 0: qubit 1 is guaranteed to be 0
Measure qubit 0 → 1: qubit 1 is guaranteed to be 1

🌊 Wave Functions & Schrödinger — Visualizing Quantum Mechanics

This section covers the Module 1 topic "Visualizing Quantum Mechanics" — understanding how quantum particles actually behave at the mathematical level. Do not panic — you just need to understand the ideas, not solve the equations.

In classical physics, if you know a ball's position and velocity, you can predict exactly where it will be at any future time using Newton's laws. Quantum mechanics is radically different. You can never know exactly where a quantum particle is. Instead, you only know the probability of finding it at different locations. This probability distribution is described by a mathematical object called the wave function.

Wave function (ψ): A mathematical description of a quantum particle's state. It is not a real, physical wave you can see — it is a probability amplitude function. The square of the wave function, |ψ(x)|², gives the probability density of finding the particle at position x. The wave function contains all information about a quantum system — its position probabilities, momentum probabilities, energy levels, everything.

In 1926, Erwin Schrödinger wrote the equation that describes how a quantum system's wave function evolves over time. It is the quantum version of Newton's second law F=ma. You do not need to know the equation — just know this:

  • The Schrödinger equation tells us how qubits evolve during computation (before measurement)
  • Quantum gates are mathematical operations that solve the Schrödinger equation for specific time periods
  • The evolution is perfectly predictable and reversible — until measurement happens
  • Measurement is where the randomness comes in — the wave function "collapses" to one outcome
The Schrödinger's Cat thought experiment: Schrödinger proposed this to illustrate the strangeness of quantum mechanics. A cat is sealed in a box with a radioactive atom that has a 50% chance of decaying in one hour. If it decays, it triggers a mechanism that releases poison and kills the cat. According to quantum mechanics, before you open the box, the atom is in superposition — both decayed AND not decayed. This means the cat is simultaneously alive AND dead. Only opening the box (measuring) forces the system to choose. Key lesson: superposition is not just for small particles — in principle it applies to everything quantum, but larger objects decohere so fast we never observe their superposition.

📏 Quantum Measurement & the No-Cloning Theorem

Measurement in quantum computing is not like checking a speedometer. It is a fundamentally disruptive act. Here is what happens step by step when you measure a qubit:

1️⃣
Wave function collapse
The qubit is in superposition α|0⟩+β|1⟩. The moment you measure it, the superposition collapses instantly to either |0⟩ or |1⟩. You cannot measure "halfway."
2️⃣
Probabilistic outcome
Which result you get is random — 0 with probability |α|² and 1 with probability |β|². You cannot predict the single outcome — only its probability.
3️⃣
Irreversible destruction
After measurement, the superposition is gone. The qubit is now a definite classical bit. You cannot un-measure it. The quantum information in the superposition is permanently lost.
4️⃣
Statistical repetition
Because each measurement gives only one bit of information, quantum algorithms are run hundreds or thousands of times (called "shots"). The frequency of 0s and 1s reveals the underlying probability distribution.
The No-Cloning Theorem: It is impossible to create a perfect copy of an unknown quantum state. Mathematically proven — not just a practical limitation. This has profound consequences:

1. You cannot copy qubit data the way you copy a file on a computer
2. Quantum information cannot be "amplified" the way classical signals can
3. This is actually a security feature — any eavesdropper trying to copy quantum communication will disturb it and be detected (basis of Quantum Key Distribution)
4. Quantum error correction must work around this — it cannot make copies, so it uses clever encoding instead
Heisenberg's Uncertainty Principle (related concept): You cannot simultaneously know both the exact position and exact momentum of a quantum particle. The more precisely you measure one, the less precisely you can know the other. This is not a limitation of instruments — it is a fundamental property of nature. In qubits, measuring in one "basis" destroys information about another basis.
🎯 Day 2 — Exam Practice Questions
Q1: What is a qubit and how does it differ from a classical bit?
A classical bit is either exactly 0 or exactly 1. A qubit is a quantum two-level system that can be in a superposition of both 0 and 1 simultaneously, described by the state α|0⟩+β|1⟩ where |α|²+|β|²=1. Only measurement forces it to a definite value.
Q2: What does the Hadamard gate do and what state does it produce from |0⟩?
The Hadamard gate creates an equal superposition. Applied to |0⟩, it produces (|0⟩+|1⟩)/√2 — a state with 50% probability of measuring 0 and 50% probability of measuring 1. On the Bloch sphere, it moves a qubit from the north pole to the equator.
Q3: How do you create a Bell state? Why is it significant?
Apply Hadamard to qubit 0, then apply CNOT with qubit 0 as control and qubit 1 as target. Result: (|00⟩+|11⟩)/√2. Significant because the two qubits are maximally entangled — measuring one instantly determines the other regardless of distance.
Q4: What is the no-cloning theorem and why does it matter?
The no-cloning theorem states that an unknown quantum state cannot be perfectly copied. This means: quantum information cannot be amplified like classical signals, eavesdropping on quantum communication is detectable (QKD security), and error correction must use encoding rather than copying.
Q5: What happens physically when you measure a qubit in superposition?
The superposition collapses — the qubit irreversibly becomes either |0⟩ or |1⟩ with probabilities |α|² and |β|² respectively. The act of measurement destroys all quantum information in the superposition. This is why quantum algorithms are run many times (shots) to reconstruct probability distributions.
